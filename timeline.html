Early Nintendo: 1889-1981

September 23rd 1889

Nintendo Koppa was founded by Fusajiro Yamauchi in Kyoto, Japan. They started out as a manufacturer of hanafuda (Japanese playing cards)

1929

Yamauchi retires from Nintendo Koppa without a son, so adopts his son-in-law to take over the business according to Japanese tradition

1933

The company is renamed Yamauchi Nintendo & Company

1950

Hiroshi Yamauchi becomes the company’s third president

1951

The name of the company is changed to Nintendo Playing Card Co. Ltd.

1953

Nintendo becomes the first company in Japan to produce playing cards made of plastic

1963

The company is renamed to Nintendo by Yamauchi. The company begins to expand its business, including Instant Rice and a remote controlled vacuum cleaner known as the Chritorie, all of which eventually fail.

1966

Gunpei Yokoi, a conveyor belt repair main for Nintendo, creates the Ultra Machine toy, an extending arm-shaped toy and is a massive success for the company. Yokoi would go on to develop the Ten Billion Barrel puzzle, and a Love Tester.

1970

Nintendo releases the first solar-powered light gun named the Nintendo Beam Gun, the first of its kind for home use

1973

The company developed a laser clay shooting system using the same technology from there Beam Gun toys for some arcade machines, which were quite successful, leading Nintendo down a new path

1974

Nintendo developed an image-projection system and employed the 16mm film projector in amusement arcades. The company began exporting these arcade machines to America and Europe. Nintendo also releases its first video arcade machine EVR Race

1980

Nintendo begins testing the handheld video game market with its fist product from the Game & Watch line. These would continue to be released until 1991, with around 60 different variants being made. Special editions are still released to this day.

1981

Shigeru Miyamoto designs Donkey Kong for the arcade, quickly becoming one of the most successful arcade games ever released. This then lead to Nintendo looking more at the Home gaming market

Retro Nintendo: 1982-2003

July 1983

The Family Computer (more commonly known as the Famicom) is released by Nintendo in Japan. It is the company’s first attempt at a cartridge-based video game console. It sells 500,000 copies within the first 2 months of release.

July 1985

The Famicom is released in North America under a new name: The Nintendo Entertainment System, more commonly known as the NES. It is released alongside well known classics such as Duck Hunt and Super Mario Bros and quickly becomes one of the bestselling consoles of all time, selling over 60 million units over its lifetime.

September 1986

The NES is released in Europe

April 1989

Nintendo’s first true step into handheld gaming is taken with the release of the GameBoy in Japan, bundled alongside Tetris. The GameBoy was different from Nintendo’s Game & Watch in that, like the NES, it used cartridges so games could be changed. Due to its easy accessibility and portability, the GameBoy went on to sell 118 million units. It would go on to be released in North America in July 1989.

November 1990

The successor of the NES, the Super Famicon (or the SNES oversees) is released in Japan. It is received very well, with all units being sold out across Japan within 3 days of release, and 1.6 million units sold by June 1991

August 1991

The SNES is released in North America, and then released in Europe in 1992.

July 1995

The doomed Virtual Boy is released in Japan by Nintendo, a sort of prototype of what would become moder Virtual Reality headsets. It was badly reviewed by critics and players alike, due to its high price point and health concerns. It was released in North America in August the same year, but never made it over to Europe, due to poor sales. It sold less than 1 million units, and was discontinued in Japan less than 6 months after its release.

June 1996

The Nintendo 64 is released in Japan, Nintendo’s first jump into true 3D gaming. It sells over half a million units on its first day alone. It is released in North America in September of the same year. The console became home to some of the most iconic games of the generation, such as Super Mario 64, Ocarina of Time, and Super Smash Bros.

October 1998

The GameBoy Colour is released in Japan, and then released in North America and Europe one month later. It improves upon its predecessor the GameBoy by including colour, along with other improved technical specifications.

March 2001

The GameBoy Advance is released in Japan, followed by North America and Europe in June that same year. This improved once again on the GameBoy line with better graphics, and was one of Nintendo’s most successful projects, selling over 1.5 million units within the first month of its release

September 2001

The GameCube is launched in Japan, releasing in November 2001 in North America, alongside some of the most popular games for the console Luigi’s Mansion and Super Smash Bros. Melee.

May 2002

Horishi Yamauchi steps down as president of Nintendo and names Satoru Iwata as his successor

'Modern' Nintendo 2004-Present

November 2004

The Nintendo DS is released as Nintendo’s new handheld. It uses dual-screens and has touch screen technology built into it.

March 2006

The Nintendo DS Lite is released in Japan, then released 3 months later in North America and Europe. It is an improved version of the original DS, being lighter and having a brighter screen. The DS becomes the most popular handheld device.

November 2006

Nintendo releases one of its most recognisable consoles to date: the Wii. It instantly becomes a fast seller with its unique motion controls, being advertised as accessible to all age groups and casual gamers, making it a popular family gift for the Christmas season.

February 2011

The Nintendo 3DS is released. Another handheld from Nintendo, but this one uses autostereoscopic 3D, meaning that it is able to create 3D effects without the use of special glasses.

November 2012

The Wii U is released, the successor to the Wii. It is one of Nintendo’s worst performing consoles selling just over 13 million units over its lifetime. Its failure is seen as being due to a confusing marketing campaign, lack of third-party support, lack of games when it launched, and a high price point.

November 2016

The NES Classic Edition is released. It is a miniature replica of the original NES with 30 built in games from the console.

March 2017

The Nintendo Switch is released. It is a combination of a home console and a handheld, comprising of a tablet like device with built in touchscreen, detachable controllers known as Joycons, plus a docking station that hooks up to the TV, allowing the console to be used at home or out and about. The console is seen as very successful, being the third bestselling console of all time selling over 125 million units to date.

September 2017

Following the success of the NES Classic Edition, the SNES Classic Edition is released. Similarly to the NES Classic Edition it is a miniature version of the SNES with 21 games preinstalled on it.

September 2019

The Nintendo Switch Lite is released. It is a ‘budget’ version of the original Switch that is purely handheld. It does not feature the Joycons and is not able to use the dock featured with the Switch. However, it still sells well and is considered a success.
